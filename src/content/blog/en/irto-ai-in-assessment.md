Every teacher training I run includes the same question: **is it okay to use AI in assessment?**

The question is understandable, but I think it's phrased wrong. The better question is: **what is AI allowed to do in assessment — and what should it never do?**

The answer isn't complicated, but it rests on one principle with no room for negotiation: the teacher decides the assessment. Always. AI can support assessment in many places, but the grade, the pass/fail decision and any conclusion about a student are not things you outsource to a language model.

## Where AI actually helps

Once the authority question is settled, AI becomes a genuinely useful assessment partner. Four uses I've found to work in my own teaching:

**Fast feedback for students.** Writing feedback is the most laborious part of assessment — not because the teacher doesn't know what to say, but because verbalizing the same things for thirty students takes time. AI drafts, the teacher checks and edits. Feedback improves because there's actually time to give it.

**Verbalizing assessment criteria.** Competence requirements and grading criteria are often written in administrative language that students struggle to understand. AI translates the criteria into the student's language: what this means in practice, what an accepted performance looks like. The teacher verifies the translation matches the original.

**Differentiation.** The same assignment at different difficulty levels, the same content in plain language, the same competence demonstrated in different ways. This is work that often goes undone for lack of time — and it's exactly where AI is at its best.

**Accessibility.** Clarifying text structure, alternative ways to complete a task, breaking instructions into steps. As a special education teacher I consider this the most underrated use of AI: it lowers the threshold for making accessible materials, because workload is no longer the obstacle.

## Where the line runs

The line is clear to me, and I hold it in every training:

> AI does not assess the student. It helps the teacher assess.

In practice this means three things. AI does not give grades — it can draft justifications, but the decision is made by the teacher who knows the student's situation. AI does not draw conclusions about students — it doesn't judge motivation, learning difficulties or support needs. And no student personal data goes into AI tools — feedback and drafts are produced with minimal data, without names or identifiers.

The third point is the one that surprises people most often. A good rule of thumb: if the text could be posted on a bulletin board, it can go to an AI. If it couldn't, it can't.

## The framework that holds it together

A line alone isn't enough — you also need a structure that keeps assessment aligned. I use two tools teachers already know.

**Constructive alignment**: goals, teaching and assessment tell the same story. If AI is used to produce feedback, that feedback must be based on the same criteria the grade is based on — not on the language model's own idea of good work. That's why the criteria are always given to the AI, never invented by it.

**Bloom's taxonomy**: what level of thinking the assessment actually measures. In the AI era this matters more than ever, because recall- and repetition-level tasks are solved by a language model in seconds. An assessment that only measures the lowest levels no longer measures the student. AI can help lift tasks to the analysis and evaluation levels — and reveal, along the way, how much of your current task bank a machine can solve.

## The checklist

When I design assessment that uses AI, I go through five questions:

1. **Who decides?** If the answer isn't "the teacher", the plan goes back to the drawing board.
2. **What criteria is the feedback based on?** The criteria are given to the AI — it doesn't get to invent its own.
3. **What data goes to the AI?** No names, no identifiers, no sensitive information.
4. **What level of thinking does the task measure?** If a language model solves it in seconds, it doesn't measure the student.
5. **Does the student know how AI was used?** Transparency is part of trustworthy assessment.

The list is deliberately short. Its job is not to turn AI use into bureaucracy, but to make sure the benefit comes from the right place: the teacher's time, not the teacher's responsibility.

## What to take away

AI doesn't change the fundamentals of assessment. Constructive alignment, clear criteria and the teacher's professional judgment were the backbone of good assessment before language models, and they still are.

What AI changes is how the work is distributed: verbalizing, drafting and editing get faster, freeing the teacher's time for the part of assessment a machine cannot do — understanding the student's situation and making the decision.

The authority is not negotiable. Everything else is.
